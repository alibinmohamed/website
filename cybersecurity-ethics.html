MODULE 01 · WHY

Why ethics?

You're starting a career where you'll have privileged access to other people's computers and data. Ethics is the simple question that follows: when you have that power, how should you use it?

Ethics and law often agree, but not always. Reading a friend's email is both unethical and illegal. Selling someone's location data is legal in many countries but obviously not ok. The whole rest of this course exists because “it's legal” isn't enough — you also need an answer to “is it right?”

A useful test

Before you do something on a computer that isn't yours, ask: would I be comfortable if this showed up on tomorrow's news with my name attached? If the answer is no, stop.

What “ethical hacking” means

You'll hear the phrase a lot. It only means one thing: attacking a system with written permission from its owner, inside an agreed scope, to help that owner. Without permission, the same actions are a crime — we'll see exactly which laws in Chapter 5.

The next chapter answers the obvious follow-up: who decides what “right” means in this profession?

MODULE 02 · PROMISES

Your code of honour

Chapter 1 said you need ethics. Chapter 2 answers: who actually writes them down? The profession does. Every certificate you'll ever earn — CISSP, CEH, OSCP, ACM membership — is signed under a short written code. Break the code, lose the credential.

(ISC)² — four canons, in order

  1. Protect society and the public.
  2. Act honourably, honestly, justly, responsibly, legally.
  3. Serve your clients well.
  4. Look after the profession.

The order matters. If a paying client (Canon 3) asks you to do something that hurts the public (Canon 1), the public wins.

ACM (2018)

The Association for Computing Machinery's code opens with one sentence you can memorise: “Contribute to society and to human well-being.” Everything else — honesty, fairness, privacy, competent work — follows from that.

The pattern

Read enough of these codes (ACM, (ISC)², IEEE-CS, EC-Council) and you'll notice they all rank the public first, then honesty, then your client, then yourself. That's the pattern of security ethics in one line.

Codes say “protect the public.” Chapter 3 asks: protect what exactly?

MODULE 03 · WHAT WE PROTECT

What we actually protect

The codes from Chapter 2 say “protect the public.” In a security context, “protect” means three specific things, called the CIA triad:

LetterPlain EnglishBroken when…
C — ConfidentialityOnly the right people can read it.A password database is leaked.
I — IntegrityThe data is correct and hasn't been changed.A prescription record is altered.
A — AvailabilityThe system is up when people need it.A DDoS knocks a hospital site offline.

That's it. Every breach you'll ever read about violates at least one of those three letters.

One more thing: privacy

CIA covers data about the system. Privacy covers data about people: their names, locations, health records, browsing history. NIST treats privacy as its own concern with the NIST Privacy Framework, exactly because it can be violated even when CIA is intact.

Chapter 3 told you what to protect. Chapter 4 tells you what the law says you must protect when the data belongs to people.

MODULE 04 · RULES ABOUT DATA

The rules about data

Chapter 3 introduced privacy. Chapter 4 is the legal version of the same idea: when the data is about people, here is what the law says you must do. Three names cover most of what you'll meet.

GDPR — the European baseline

The General Data Protection Regulation (EU 2016/679) protects anyone physically in the EU, no matter where the company is. The headline numbers to remember: tell users why you're collecting their data, let them ask for it back or have it deleted, and notify the regulator within 72 hours if you lose it.

HIPAA — American health data

HIPAA (1996) protects “Protected Health Information” in the United States: anything a hospital, insurer or doctor records about you.

Bahrain PDPL — Law No. 30 of 2018

Bahrain's own privacy law, in force since 1 August 2019. It looks a lot like GDPR: a lawful reason for collecting data, consent for sensitive categories, limits on cross-border transfer, and a regulator (the Personal Data Protection Authority) that can fine you.

Privacy laws control how data may be used. Chapter 5 covers the other half: what acts against systems are crimes.

MODULE 05 · RULES ABOUT ACTS

The rules about acts

Three statutes, three countries, one shared idea: touching a computer you don't have permission for is a crime.

  • U.S. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA, 1986). Bans accessing a computer without authorisation, or going beyond what you were authorised to do.
  • U.K. Computer Misuse Act 1990. Section 1 makes unauthorised access an offence. Sections 2 and 3 add intent and damage on top.
  • Bahrain Decree-Law No. 60 of 2014. Bahrain's cybercrime law. Same pattern: unauthorised access, electronic fraud, attacks on national infrastructure — all criminal.
Practical line: running a port-scan or vulnerability scan against a server you don't own and haven't been told you may test is, under all three of these laws, a crime. “I was just curious” is not a defence.

That single rule — “don't touch what you don't have permission for” — is the bridge to Chapter 6, where we look at how permission actually works in practice.

MODULE 06 · PERMISSION

Permission is everything

Chapter 5 ended with the line: don't touch what you don't have permission for. So how do real penetration testers stay on the right side of that line every day? With paper.

What you need before you start

  1. Rules of Engagement (RoE). Which systems are in scope, which techniques are allowed, what hours, who to call if something breaks.
  2. Authorisation letter. A signature from someone with the authority to bind the target organisation.
  3. NDA protecting the customer's data.

That short stack of paper is the only thing separating you from the offences in Chapter 5. NIST writes this up as SP 800-115; you'll see the same advice in every professional curriculum.

Bug-bounty programmes & “Safe Harbor”

When you can't get a private contract, public bug-bounty programmes (HackerOne, Bugcrowd, Intigriti) play the same role. Their Safe Harbor clause says: “if you stick to our scope and rules, we won't pursue you legally.” Read the rules before you press a key.

Once you have permission and you go looking, sooner or later you'll actually find a vulnerability. Chapter 7 picks up exactly there.

MODULE 07 · WHAT TO DO WITH A BUG

What to do when you find a bug

You've earned permission, you've tested, and now you've found something serious. Who do you tell, when, and how much?

Three ways to disclose

  • Full disclosure — publish immediately. Helps defenders, but also hands the bug to attackers.
  • Non-disclosure — keep it secret or sell it. Leaves the public exposed.
  • Coordinated disclosure — tell the vendor first, give them a fair window to patch, then publish. This is what the industry has settled on.

Numbers worth memorising

  • 90 days — Google Project Zero's standard deadline.
  • ISO/IEC 29147 — the international standard for receiving and handling vulnerability reports.
  • CVE — “Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures”. The unique public ID (e.g. CVE-2024-3094) every known vulnerability gets. Run by MITRE.

Chapter 7 was about respecting the people who run the systems. Chapter 8 is about respecting the people who wrote the code in the first place.

MODULE 08 · OWNERSHIP

Code, ideas, and ownership

You'll write code and use other people's code every single day. Chapter 8 is the short version of the rules around who owns what in software.

Four kinds of ownership

  • Copyright — protects the code you wrote.
  • Patent — protects an invention.
  • Trademark — protects a name or logo.
  • Trade secret — protects information you keep confidential.

Open-source licences in one minute

  • Permissive (MIT, Apache 2.0) — “do whatever you want, just keep my name on it.”
  • Copyleft (GPL) — “if you ship a product based on my code, your product has to be open too.”
  • Proprietary / EULA — you bought a licence to use, not the code itself.

Picking the wrong licence in a school project is awkward; picking the wrong licence in a startup is expensive. When in doubt, read it.

Chapters 1–8 are settled ground. Chapter 9 is where ethics is still being figured out.

MODULE 09 · THE NEW FRONTIER

The new frontier

Chapters 1–8 had clear answers. Chapter 9 is where the rules are still being written — and where you'll spend most of your career.

AI

AI systems can fail in ways no human attacker would: a hiring model that's biased against women, a face-recognition system that misidentifies people of colour, a chatbot that confidently invents medical advice. The EU AI Act (2024) is the first big law to address it, sorting AI systems by risk: unacceptable, high, limited, minimal. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023) is the matching voluntary playbook.

Surveillance

In 2013 Edward Snowden disclosed that the NSA was running mass-surveillance programmes (PRISM and others) that were technically lawful but politically and ethically explosive. The point isn't whether Snowden was right — it's that lawful and ethical aren't always the same word, exactly as we said in Chapter 1.

Where you fit in

The codes from Chapter 2 still apply. The CIA triad from Chapter 3 still applies. The “permission is everything” rule from Chapter 6 still applies. The novelty in this chapter is that the next big ethics question — about AI, about privacy, about whistleblowing — will be answered by people in your generation. Maybe by you.

Capstone — Write the Club's Code of Conduct

You've walked from “why ethics?” to “the new frontier” in nine short chapters. Pull it together by writing a one-page Code of Conduct for the UTB Cybersecurity Club that uses ideas from every chapter:

  • Open with the order of duties from Chapter 2 (public, honesty, client, colleagues, self).
  • State what “authorised testing” means inside Club labs and outside them, citing the laws in Chapter 5.
  • Say how members will disclose any vulnerability they find, using the rules from Chapter 7.
  • Add a short review process for alleged breaches.

Send the draft to a senior Club member. A signed Code of Conduct earns you a place in the Club's organised pen-tests and bug-bounty triage events.
